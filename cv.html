Projects

  • cr.churchfinder: A WIP fullstack webapp that a friend down in Costa Rica suggested. The aim is to have a form that users can fill out that will locate churches within their desired radius given a few constraints. I plan to leverage OpenStreetMaps (OSM) to display maps and occasionally update the webapp's database. However, OSM doesn't have a lot of information on churches in Costa Rica, so I will be adding a system to allow authorized users (some pastors and administrators that I've worked with in Costa Rica) to add and remove churches to the system. I also plan to add these locations to OSM and contribute back to the project in this manner. Tech Stack: Python, Django, PostgreSQL, and OSM.
  • CityByte: As part of CSC 510, my team of three overhauled the front end, added in Google sign-ups/sign-ins, implemented maps for users to view locations of interest, and introduced an itinerary for users to plan out their trips.
  • Food Menu Optimization: One of the two non-CompSci classes I took in graduate school was ISE 501: Introductions to Operations Research. For the project in this class, my team chose to research and implement an optimization of the McDonalds Menu from a health perspective with health constraints (e.g. calories).
  • Quantum Computing: My teammate and I explored and utilized IBM's mid-circuit resets on some of their Quantum Computers in order to find a secret string. We used the BV algorithm as a basis for our computations, but reset both qubits after each qubit after computing the answer for each bit of the secret string. This increased the run time from O(1) to O(n), however it decreased the qubits needed to solve from n to 2. This is actually a decent trade-off given quantum computers currently do not have many qubits. (Slides) However, if you are solving in O(n) time, you may as well use a classical computer.

Classes Taken

A non-exhaustive list of CompSci/SWE courses that were formative to me (along with one or two that were just plain fun). I've marked 'U' or 'G' to indicate if they were taken at the undergraduate or graduate level respectively (mostly because graduate courses required a survey/paper and/or research of some kind).

Software Engineering (U/G)

NC State focuses heavily on SWE, so below are the descriptions of a few SWE courses. From recollection, I've received an A, A+, or "A++" (>100 although officially just an A+) in all my SWE courses.

  • CSC 216: an introductory yet intense SWE course that teaches you the basics of OOP (via Java), utilizing test cases, debugging, and a few best-practices.
  • CSC 326: a more in-depth SWE class that focuses out SWE best practices (e.g. git/revision control, working with teams, documentation, {black, white} box testing, etc.), the design of systems, and of course implementing designs. This was one of my favorite classes.
  • CSC 510: a graduate-level software engineering class that covered everything from the conceptual to the nitty-gritty (and somewhat niche parts) of SWE. Two major takeaways from experiences in this class was 1) acclimating to codebases quickly, utilizing them, and improving upon them and 2) teaching, equipping, and encouraging those newer to or less proficient in SWE.

Quantum Computing (G)

An introductory quantum computing (QC) class covering basic quantum physics and theory, QC architecture, QC programming, and the analysis, design, and implementation of QC algorithms on simulated and actual quantum computers with a focus on real-world application.

Automated Learning & Data Analysis (G)

A course that combined the fields of data science and machine learning with the application of utilizing machine learning and conducting data mining.

Content, Assessment, and Pedagogy (G)

One of my favorite classes at NC State! We learned and discussed various (proposed) "methods" of learning, how to structure material for effective understanding, and how to assess the process of education in order to improve the cycle of learning.

Software Security (U)

A very fun class that covered the essentials about secure software development. Topics included the classic CIA triad, threat analysis and planning, the security ecosystem (CVEs, MITRE), useful tools (Wireshark, fuzzers), and web exploitation. TL;DR: blue team.

Computer Security (U)

Don't let the name fool you, this was more-so a class on exploitation/hacking (although naming it 'Computer Hacking' probably wouldn't pass muster with the powers that be). We covered a ton of topics in this course from hardware exploits to reverse engineering to "softer" concepts such as the going-rate for different vulnerabilities. Many of the assignments focused on gaining privileged access to systems, sort of like mini-CTFs but with one topic of focus at a time.

Network Security (U)

This class had surprisingly little to do with networks once we finished a short summary of the TCP/IP layers and primarily focused on encryption, the CIA triad (at a higher level than SWSec), firewalls, automation, and the like. From this class, I discovered the Open Source Security Podcast which I highly recommend.

Computer Networks (G)

The course covered concepts such as TCP/IP in-depth, error checking and correction, and the mathematics and implementation of various forms of wireless communication (wifi/802.11b, 5G, multiplexing, etc.). As part of this class, I completed a topical survey with a team on Disconnected, Intermittent, Limited (DIL) environments which has utility in military and emergency situations.

Data Structures and Algorithms (U/G)

For the undergraduate class, we went over the analysis, design, and implementation of various algorithms (e.g., bubble sort, merge sort, radix sort, etc.) and data structures & related concepts (e.g., linked lists, queuing, concatenation, etc.). I will be taking the graduate-level version of this course in the Spring of 2025.
